\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in role-playing tasks, yet maintaining consistent character personalities across \textit{extended multi-turn interactions} remains challenging. We propose \textbf{PersonaForge}, a framework that combines \textbf{(1) a three-layer personality architecture} grounded in psychological theory and \textbf{(2) a dual-process generation mechanism} inspired by cognitive science. Our three-layer model captures personality at three levels: \textbf{Core Traits} (Big Five personality dimensions and psychological defense mechanisms), \textbf{Speaking Style} (linguistic patterns), and \textbf{Dynamic State} (mood, energy, and evolving relationships). The dual-process mechanism selectively activates a \textit{Think-then-Speak} pipeline for critical interactions. Experiments on 37 characters from classical Chinese literature demonstrate that PersonaForge achieves: (1) +18.5\% personality consistency (PC, absolute 0--1 score) validated via pairwise LLM-as-Judge with $r=0.82$ human correlation, (2) significantly reduced character drift over 50-turn conversations (drift rate 8.2\% vs. 31.7\% baseline), and (3) +42.9\% defense mechanism manifestation. Human evaluation confirms that characters exhibit more authentic and psychologically coherent behaviors. Cross-domain pilot on 8 additional characters from English literature suggests generalizability.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Role-playing with Large Language Models (LLMs) has emerged as a compelling application, enabling immersive storytelling and virtual companionship \citep{chen2024persona}. However, maintaining consistent character personalities across extended multi-turn interactions remains a fundamental challenge. Current approaches often suffer from:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Character drift}: Personality traits gradually shift or become inconsistent over long conversations
    \item \textbf{Shallow personality modeling}: Reliance on surface-level text descriptions without psychological grounding
    \item \textbf{Style instability}: Language patterns fluctuate based on prompt variations
    \item \textbf{Static representations}: Inability to model dynamic emotional states
\end{enumerate}

To address these limitations, we propose \textbf{PersonaForge}, a framework integrating personality psychology and cognitive science. Our key insight is that \textbf{different aspects of personality collapse require different architectural solutions}---no single mechanism suffices. Our contributions:

\paragraph{Three-Layer Personality Architecture.} Unlike prior prompt-based approaches that conflate personality dimensions into unstructured descriptions, we introduce a \textbf{functionally decomposed} architecture where each layer addresses a \textbf{distinct, empirically non-redundant} failure mode:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Core Traits} (Big Five \citep{mccrae1992introduction} + Defense Mechanisms \citep{vaillant1994ego}): Prevents \textit{trait drift}---without explicit trait anchoring, LLMs gradually shift personality over long contexts.
    \item \textbf{Speaking Style Matrix}: Prevents \textit{style collapse}---linguistic patterns are orthogonal to traits; a character can maintain personality while losing distinctive voice.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic State}: Prevents \textit{context blindness}---static profiles cannot adapt to evolving emotional and relational contexts.
\end{itemize}
Crucially, \textbf{no subset of layers is sufficient}: ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) shows removing each layer degrades a \textit{different} metric, confirming the architecture is not merely ``prompt stacking'' but captures complementary aspects of personality.

\paragraph{Selective Dual-Process Generation.} Inspired by Kahneman's dual-system theory \citep{kahneman2011thinking}, we implement \textit{Think-then-Speak} that activates only for critical interactions ($\approx$38\% of turns), achieving 96\% of full dual-process performance at 89\% cost (Section~\ref{sec:trigger}). This cognitive-economic framing provides a principled solution to the efficiency-quality tradeoff.

\paragraph{Rigorous Multi-Evaluator Validation.} We address LLM-as-Judge limitations through: (1) \textbf{50-turn long-dialogue benchmark} with drift/recovery metrics, (2) \textbf{multi-model evaluation} (GPT-4/Claude/Qwen) with $r \geq 0.84$ cross-judge correlation, (3) \textbf{cross-generator validation} showing consistent +10--11\% improvement across GPT-4/Qwen/Llama backends, and (4) \textbf{human expert validation} including blind annotation protocols.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{LLM-Based Role-Playing}

Recent work has explored various approaches to character-consistent generation. \citet{shao2023character} introduced Character-LLM, fine-tuning models on character data. \citet{wang2023rolellm} proposed RoleLLM with retrieval-augmented generation and a comprehensive benchmark (RoleBench). \citet{wang2024incharacter} presented InCharacter, evaluating personality fidelity through psychological interviews. \citet{chen2024bookworld} developed BookWorld for multi-agent narrative simulation. CharacterGLM \citep{zhou2023characterglm} uses persona-based fine-tuning for Chinese conversational AI. For a comprehensive survey of role-playing agents, see \citet{chen2024persona}.

While these methods advance the field, they typically rely on unstructured descriptions and lack psychological grounding. Our work introduces structured personality representations based on established psychological models.

\subsection{Persona Dialogue and Memory Systems}

The Persona-Chat dataset \citep{zhang2018personalizing} established persona-conditioned dialogue as a benchmark task. \citet{wolf2019transfertransfo} demonstrated transfer learning for persona-based agents. For long-context management, retrieval-augmented generation \citep{lewis2020rag} enables knowledge-intensive tasks. MemGPT \citep{packer2023memgpt} extends LLMs with operating system-like memory management. \citet{komeili2022internet} demonstrated internet-augmented dialogue generation.

\subsection{Agent Architectures}

\citet{park2023generative} demonstrated generative agents with memory and reflection for simulating human behavior. CAMEL \citep{li2023camel} explores multi-agent communication for emergent behaviors. AutoGen \citep{wu2023autogen} enables flexible multi-agent conversations.

\subsection{Personality Modeling in NLP}

The Big Five personality model \citep{mccrae1992introduction,mccrae1987validation,costa1992four} has been applied to sentiment analysis \citep{mairesse2007using}, dialogue generation \citep{zhong2020towards}, and author profiling \citep{schwartz2013personality}. Cross-cultural validation remains important \citep{schmitt2007geographic}. However, prior work rarely integrates defense mechanisms---a core clinical psychology concept describing stress responses \citep{vaillant1994ego,vaillant1986empirically,vaillant1976theoretical}. The Defense Style Questionnaire \citep{bond1989determination,sammallahti1996short} and recent hierarchical assessments \citep{prout2021hierarchy} provide empirical foundations we adapt for annotation.

\subsection{Dual-Process Theory and Reasoning}

Kahneman's dual-system theory \citep{kahneman2011thinking} distinguishes fast (System 1) and slow (System 2) thinking, with theoretical refinements by \citet{evans2013dual}. In NLP, this inspired chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain}, self-consistency \citep{wang2023selfconsistency}, ReAct \citep{yao2023react}, and Reflexion \citep{shinn2023reflexion}. We extend this paradigm to role-playing by modeling internal cognition vs. external expression.

\subsection{Three-Layer Personality Architecture}

Our architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}) models personality at three distinct levels, each serving a specific function in maintaining character consistency.

\subsubsection{Core Traits Layer}

\paragraph{Big Five Dimensions.} Each character has scores $\mathbf{b} = (o, c, e, a, n) \in [0,1]^5$ for Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism. These scores directly influence inner monologue generation (see Section~\ref{sec:inner}).

\paragraph{Defense Mechanisms.} Based on Vaillant's hierarchy \citep{vaillant1994ego}, we model defense mechanisms as \textbf{context-sensitive preferences} rather than fixed labels. Each character has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{primary mechanism} most likely to activate under stress
    \item Explicit \textbf{None} when no stressor is detected
\end{itemize}
Activation follows the decision tree in Appendix~\ref{sec:dmguide}: mechanisms only manifest when a stressor is present in the interaction context. This models the psychological reality that defense mechanisms are situationally triggered \citep{vaillant1986empirically}. The nine supported mechanisms are: Rationalization, Projection, Denial, Sublimation, Displacement, Humor, Intellectualization, Repression, and Reaction Formation.

\subsubsection{Speaking Style Layer}

We define a style matrix $\mathbf{S} = \{l, v, p, \mathbf{e}, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{t}\}$ with the following operational definitions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $l \in \{\texttt{short}, \texttt{medium}, \texttt{long}, \texttt{mixed}\}$: Sentence length preference. We define \texttt{short} as avg $<$10 words, \texttt{medium} as 10--20 words, \texttt{long} as $>$20 words.
    \item $v \in \{\texttt{academic}, \texttt{casual}, \texttt{network}, \texttt{mixed}\}$: Vocabulary register. \texttt{academic} uses formal/literary terms; \texttt{casual} uses colloquial speech; \texttt{network} includes internet slang.
    \item $p \in \{\texttt{minimal}, \texttt{standard}, \texttt{excessive}, \texttt{mixed}\}$: Punctuation habits including ellipsis frequency, exclamation ratio, and special markers (e.g., ``$\sim\sim$'').
    \item $\mathbf{e}$: Emoji usage specification with \texttt{frequency} $\in \{\texttt{none}, \texttt{low}, \texttt{medium}, \texttt{high}\}$, \texttt{preferred} emojis, and \texttt{avoided} emojis.
    \item $\mathbf{c}$: Up to 5 character-specific catchphrases extracted from source text.
    \item $\mathbf{t}$: Tone markers (e.g., sentence-final particles, hedging words, intensifiers) as keyword list.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Dynamic State Layer}

The state $\mathbf{D}_t = \{m_t, \epsilon_t, \mathbf{R}_t\}$ tracks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $m_t$: Current mood $\in \{\texttt{cheerful}, \texttt{neutral}, \texttt{melancholy}\}$
    \item $\epsilon_t$: Energy level $\in [0, 100]$
    \item $\mathbf{R}_t$: Relationship map where $\mathbf{R}_t[\text{entity}] = \{\text{intimacy} \in [0,100], \text{history\_summary}\}$
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{State Update Rules.} After each turn $t$, we apply Algorithm~\ref{alg:update}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sentiment Analysis}: We use \textbf{LLM-based structured extraction} rather than keyword matching. The LLM outputs JSON: \texttt{\{sentiment, stressor\_present, emotion\}} with self-consistency voting over 3 samples.
    \item \textbf{Mood Update}: $m_{t+1} = f_\text{mood}(m_t, s_t)$ following a mood transition matrix (e.g., \texttt{neutral} + \texttt{positive} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{cheerful})
    \item \textbf{Energy Decay}: $\epsilon_{t+1} = \max(0, \epsilon_t + \delta)$ where $\delta = +10$ if \texttt{positive}, $\delta = -15$ if \texttt{negative}, $\delta = -2$ otherwise
    \item \textbf{Relationship Update}: If interlocutor identified, $\text{intimacy}_{t+1} = \text{clip}(\text{intimacy}_t + \Delta, 0, 100)$ where $\Delta = +5$ if \texttt{positive}, $\Delta = -3$ if \texttt{negative}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dual-Process Generation}

\subsubsection{Selective Activation}
\label{sec:trigger}

We define ``critical interactions'' requiring dual-process activation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First Encounter}: Interlocutor role code not in $\mathbf{R}_t$
    \item \textbf{Emotional Content}: Presence of emotion keywords (e.g., ``爱''/``love'', ``恨''/``hate'', ``生气''/``angry'')
    \item \textbf{Core Interest Trigger}: Topic overlaps with character's interest tags
    \item \textbf{Significant Relationship Change}: Previous interaction caused $|\Delta\text{intimacy}| \geq 3$
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Cognitive-Economic Framing.} Following \citet{kahneman2011thinking}'s insight that System 2 thinking is ``effortful'' while System 1 is ``automatic,'' we frame selective activation as a \textbf{resource allocation optimization problem}. Let $c_1$ denote the cost (tokens) of System 1 (direct response) and $c_2$ the cost of System 2 (Think-then-Speak). Let $u(x)$ be the utility (personality consistency) gained from processing interaction $x$. Our mechanism solves:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\pi} \sum_{x} \pi(x) \cdot u_2(x) + (1-\pi(x)) \cdot u_1(x) - \lambda \cdot c(\pi)
\end{equation}
where $\pi(x) \in \{0,1\}$ is the activation decision, $u_2(x) > u_1(x)$ for critical interactions, and $\lambda$ controls the cost-quality trade-off. Our trigger heuristics approximate the optimal $\pi^*$ by identifying high-$\Delta u$ interactions (where $\Delta u = u_2 - u_1$ is largest).

This yields $\approx$38\% trigger rate with 96\% of full dual-process performance (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}), achieving near-optimal utility at 89\% of the cost.

\paragraph{Trigger Diagnostics.} On a held-out set of 200 labeled interactions (100 critical, 100 non-critical), our rule-based trigger achieves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision}: 100\% (when triggered, 100\% are genuinely critical)
    \item \textbf{Recall}: 75\% (captures 75\% of critical interactions)
    \item \textbf{F1 Score}: 85.7\%
\end{itemize}
Per-category analysis: First Encounter detection achieves 100\% recall, Interest-based triggers achieve 85.7\% recall, while Emotional Content detection achieves 50\% recall (limited by keyword coverage). The conservative precision-focused design ensures that non-critical triggers incur minimal cost overhead while critical interactions receive thorough dual-process treatment.


\subsubsection{Phase 1: Inner Monologue}
\label{sec:inner}

Given Big Five scores, defense mechanism, mood, and energy, we generate internal thoughts using the prompt template in Appendix~\ref{sec:prompts}. Key generation rules:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High neuroticism ($n > 0.7$): Focus on potential threats, anxious thoughts
    \item Low agreeableness ($a < 0.4$): Allow internal criticism, honest reactions
    \item High extraversion ($e > 0.7$): More proactive, positive framing
    \item Low energy ($\epsilon < 30$): Shorter, more fatigued thoughts
    \item Stressor present: Activate defense mechanism-consistent cognition
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Phase 2: Styled Response}

The inner monologue is transformed into external speech following the style matrix $\mathbf{S}$. We provide up to 5 few-shot examples from the character's source text (stored in \texttt{style\_examples} field). The transformation prompt enforces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sentence length constraints
    \item Required catchphrase usage
    \item Appropriate tone markers
    \item Prohibition of ``translation-like'' or overly formal vocabulary (for casual characters)
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Dataset and Annotation}

We use 37 characters from \textbf{A Dream of Red Mansions} (12) and \textbf{Romance of the Three Kingdoms} (25).

\paragraph{Annotation Protocol.} Three expert annotators independently annotated each character. Big Five scores use 5-point scales (converted to $[0,1]$). Defense mechanisms selected from 9 types using a coding guide adapted from \citet{vaillant1994ego} (see Appendix~\ref{sec:dmguide}).

\paragraph{Inter-Annotator Agreement.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Big Five: Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.76$ (substantial)
    \item Defense mechanism: $\kappa = 0.82$ (almost perfect)
    \item Speaking style: $\kappa = 0.74$ (substantial)
    \item \textbf{Overall}: $\kappa = 0.78$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Tasks}

\paragraph{Task 1: Scenario-Based (8 scenarios).} Short interactions across emotional, conflict, casual, first-encounter, and decision scenarios.

\paragraph{Task 2: Long-Dialogue Benchmark (50 turns).} Each of 10 sampled characters engages in 50-turn conversations with a simulated interlocutor (GPT-4 Turbo playing a neutral questioner) introducing topic shifts, conflicts, and reconciliations at turns 15, 30, and 45. We measure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Drift Rate}: Percentage of turns where PC drops below 0.6 (human-validated threshold)
    \item \textbf{PC Trend}: Sliding-window (5-turn) average PC over conversation
    \item \textbf{Recovery Rate}: Percentage of characters recovering PC $\geq$ 0.7 within 5 turns after perturbation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}

\paragraph{Token Budget Fairness.} All baselines use 500--650 prompt tokens for persona description. For PersonaForge:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Prompt tokens} (persona + context): 600--700 tokens
    \item \textbf{Inner monologue} (when triggered): $\sim$150 tokens (not shown to user, but counted in API cost)
    \item \textbf{External response}: $\sim$100 tokens
\end{itemize}
With 38\% trigger rate, average total tokens = $600 + 0.38 \times 150 + 100 = 757$ tokens, a 16\% overhead vs. baselines.

We compare with:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vanilla LLM}: Description only
    \item \textbf{Character-LLM} \citep{shao2023character}: Description + few-shot
    \item \textbf{Structured-CoT}: Structured persona + chain-of-thought
    \item \textbf{RAG-Persona}: Retrieval-augmented with memory bank
    \item \textbf{RoleLLM-style} \citep{wang2023rolellm}: We implement a RoleLLM-inspired baseline using their role-profile-guiding prompting strategy with retrieval from character dialogues, adapted to our experimental setting
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{RAG-Persona Configuration.} The RAG baseline uses:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Retrieval corpus}: Character dialogue from source novels (avg 200 utterances/character)
    \item \textbf{Embedding}: BGE-Small (same as ours)
    \item \textbf{Top-k}: 3 most similar past utterances
    \item \textbf{Update}: Memory bank updated after each turn
    \item \textbf{Note}: RAG does NOT include our structured Big Five/DM profile
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Metric Clarifications.} Throughout this paper: (1) \textbf{PC} refers to absolute LLM-as-Judge scores (0--1 scale) unless otherwise specified as ``pairwise win-rate''; (2) Percentage improvements (e.g., +18.5\%) are relative: $(\text{PC}_{\text{ours}} - \text{PC}_{\text{baseline}}) / \text{PC}_{\text{baseline}}$.

\paragraph{Personality Consistency (PC).}

\textit{Pairwise LLM-as-Judge}: Given two responses (A/B) from different methods for the same input, GPT-4 Turbo selects which better matches the character's personality profile. Win rate computed over 200 pairs per method comparison.

\textit{Absolute LLM-as-Judge}: 1--5 rating normalized to $[0,1]$ with structured rubric (Appendix~\ref{sec:rubric}).

\textit{Validation}: On a 50-sample human-annotated set:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Pairwise agreement with human: 84.2\%
    \item Absolute score Pearson $r = 0.82$ with human ratings
    \item Judge correctly rejects 98\% of adversarial keyword-stuffed responses
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Multi-Evaluator Consistency.} To mitigate closed-loop bias, we conduct cross-evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-4 Turbo} (primary judge)
    \item \textbf{Claude 3 Opus}: Pearson $r=0.89$ with GPT-4 judgments
    \item \textbf{Qwen-72B-Chat}: Pearson $r=0.84$ with GPT-4 judgments
\end{itemize}
All three judges prefer PersonaForge over Structured-CoT at similar rates (72.3\%, 69.8\%, 71.1\%), confirming improvements are not artifacts of model-specific biases.

\paragraph{Length Control Analysis.} To ensure the judge doesn't favor longer responses: (1) truncate all responses to 100 characters and re-evaluate (correlation $\rho=0.94$ with full-length), (2) include response length as covariate in regression (coefficient not significant, $p=0.34$).

\paragraph{Style Adherence (SA).} Composite of sentence length match, catchphrase presence, tone marker usage, and vocabulary classifier score. The vocabulary classifier is a BERT-base model fine-tuned on 5,000 sentences from source texts for 37-way character identification, using leave-one-character-out validation.

\paragraph{Response Diversity (RD).} Measures lexical diversity across responses for the same character in different scenarios, computed as type-token ratio (TTR) with length normalization. Higher RD indicates the character can respond diversely while maintaining personality.

\paragraph{Defense Mechanism Appropriateness (DMA).} We evaluate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Activation Precision}: When DM manifested, is stressor present? (Target: $>$90\%)
    \item \textbf{Manifestation Accuracy}: When stressor present, does DM match profile?
\end{itemize}
LLM-as-Judge classifies stressor presence and DM type. To avoid relying solely on LLM judgments, we conducted additional \textbf{human expert validation}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Two psychology graduate students independently annotated 100 ``stressor-triggered'' turns
    \item They labeled: (a) stressor presence, (b) whether DM was exhibited, (c) DM type
    \item LLM-as-Judge achieves $\kappa=0.73$ with human experts on stressor detection and $\kappa=0.69$ on DM type classification
    \item PersonaForge achieves 87\% precision / 82\% recall on human-annotated DM labels (vs. 61\% / 54\% for Structured-CoT)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Blind Annotation Protocol.} To reduce confirmation bias, we conducted a follow-up blind study: 2 annotators judged 50 response pairs \textbf{without seeing the character's DM profile}. They labeled only whether they perceived any defense mechanism and classified its type. Agreement with profile-informed judgments: $\kappa = 0.68$, confirming that DM manifestations are perceptible even without priming.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{Evaluation Bias Controls.} Before presenting results, we summarize our measures against common LLM-as-Judge pitfalls: (1) \textbf{Closed-loop bias}: We use multi-evaluator validation (GPT-4/Claude/Qwen, $r \geq 0.84$) and human correlation ($r = 0.82$); (2) \textbf{Length bias}: Truncation analysis shows $\rho = 0.94$ correlation with full-length judgments, and length is not a significant regression covariate ($p = 0.34$); (3) \textbf{Generator-judge alignment}: Cross-generator experiments (Qwen/Llama as generators, GPT-4 as judge) show consistent +10--11\% improvement; (4) \textbf{Adversarial robustness}: Judge correctly rejects 98\% of keyword-stuffed responses; (5) \textbf{State extraction dependency}: Replacing GPT-4 extraction with BERT-base sentiment increases drift from 8.2\% to 12.4\% (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}), showing the method degrades gracefully without closed-source components. These controls substantially mitigate concerns about evaluation validity.

\paragraph{Layer-Specific Contributions.} The ablation reveals distinct functional roles that justify our three-layer design:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Big Five is essential for PC}: Removing Big Five causes the largest PC drop (-0.12), confirming core personality traits as the foundation of character consistency.

\begin{itemize}

This suggests that structured LLM extraction with self-consistency is important but not irreplaceable; simpler alternatives incur moderate performance penalties.

\subsection{Defense Mechanism Analysis}

\paragraph{DM Ablation with Human Evaluation.} To verify defense mechanisms are not merely ``decorative,'' we conducted a controlled ablation where Big Five and style were held constant, but DM was replaced with ``None.'' Human raters (N=3) evaluated 60 response pairs on ``psychological plausibility'' (5-point Likert):
\begin{itemize}
    \item With DM: 4.35 $\pm$ 0.42
    \item Without DM (None): 3.12 $\pm$ 0.68 ($p < 0.001$, paired t-test)
\end{itemize}
This confirms that defense mechanisms contribute meaningfully to perceived psychological authenticity.

\paragraph{DM vs. Style Disentanglement.} To verify defense mechanisms are not reducible to stylistic templates, we conducted an additional control: for 30 response pairs, we held speaking style constant (same $\mathbf{S}$) while varying DM activation (stressor-present vs. no-stressor). Human raters (N=3) were asked to identify which response exhibited stress-triggered cognitive patterns \textit{without seeing the character's DM profile}. Raters correctly identified stressor-present versions in 78\% of cases based on cognitive distortion patterns (e.g., rationalization, projection), confirming that DM contributes \textbf{semantic content} beyond surface-level style markers.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why Long-Dialogue Performance Matters.} Our 50-turn benchmark demonstrates that PersonaForge maintains personality stability even under perturbations, which is critical for real applications like interactive storytelling and virtual companions.

\paragraph{Defense Mechanism Validity.} The confusion matrix (Table~\ref{tab:confusion}) shows that PersonaForge successfully distinguishes between psychologically similar mechanisms (e.g., Rationalization vs. Intellectualization). Crucially, our context-sensitive activation ensures mechanisms only manifest when appropriate (92\% activation precision vs. 61\% baseline), validating our psychologically-grounded design.

\paragraph{Broader Impact: Beyond Role-Playing.} Our dual-process architecture with psychologically-grounded personality modeling has applications extending beyond interactive fiction. In \textbf{behavioral economics}, agents with realistic defense mechanisms (e.g., Rationalization, Denial) could simulate bounded rationality in market simulations. In \textbf{game-theoretic settings}, personality-consistent agents with dynamic trust/relationship states could model coalition formation, negotiation, and strategic deception more realistically than utility-maximizing rational agents. The cognitive-economic framing of our selective activation (Section~\ref{sec:trigger}) also suggests connections to attention allocation models in decision theory. We believe psychologically-grounded multi-agent simulation is a promising direction for computational social science.

\paragraph{Failure Analysis.}
\label{sec:failure}
To understand PersonaForge's limitations, we analyzed 15 cases where PC dropped below 0.6:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case 1: Stressor Misdetection.} Cao Cao (曹操) responding to subtle sarcasm. The LLM-based stressor detector misclassified indirect criticism as neutral, failing to activate his defense mechanism (Projection). \textit{Root cause}: Stressor detection relies on explicit emotional cues; subtle literary devices (irony, understatement) are under-detected.
    \item \textbf{Case 2: Style Constraint Conflict.} Wang Xifeng (王熙凤) in a formal negotiation. Her casual, domineering speaking style clashed with the scenario's formal register, producing unnaturally blunt responses even after inner monologue processing. \textit{Root cause}: Style matrix lacks context-sensitive register switching.
    \item \textbf{Case 3: Relationship Cold-Start.} New interlocutor with ambiguous role. Without relationship history, the model defaults to neutral processing, missing character-appropriate warmth/suspicion calibrations. \textit{Root cause}: Dynamic state initialization is simplistic.
\end{itemize}
These failures suggest future work on: (1) context-aware stressor detection, (2) adaptive style register, and (3) richer relationship priors.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Work.} 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark alignment}: We use a custom long-dialogue benchmark designed for stability evaluation. Future work should validate on community benchmarks such as RoleBench \citep{wang2023rolellm} and InCharacter \citep{wang2024incharacter} to enable direct comparison with existing methods.
    \item \textbf{Cross-cultural validity}: The Big Five and defense mechanisms originate from Western psychology. Cross-cultural validation is needed \citep{schmitt2007geographic}; our cross-domain pilot (8 English literature characters) provides only preliminary evidence.
    \item \textbf{Data domain}: Our primary evaluation uses 37 classical Chinese literary characters. While we show consistent improvements on English literature pilots, broader validation across modern/contemporary characters and non-literary domains remains future work.
    \item \textbf{LLM dependency}: We use closed-source LLMs for generation and evaluation. Our cross-generator experiments (Qwen/Llama) mitigate but do not eliminate this concern. All prompts and data will be released.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic state granularity}: The current mood model uses only 3 states; finer-grained emotion modeling is future work.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Ethics Statement}

Role-playing systems raise concerns about deception and emotional manipulation, particularly when modeling psychological defense mechanisms. We recommend:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mandatory AI disclosure}: Users must be informed they are interacting with AI
    \item \textbf{Content filters}: Block requests for harmful persona simulation
    \item \textbf{Vulnerable user protection}: Avoid deployment in mental health contexts without clinical oversight
    \item \textbf{Red-teaming conducted}: We tested adversarial prompts attempting to:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Elicit harmful advice ``in character'' (blocked by content filter)
        \item Manipulate users via defense mechanism simulation (mitigated by contextual activation requiring genuine stressors)
        \item Impersonate real individuals (out of scope: fictional characters only)
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
Our character data uses public-domain literary figures only.

\section{Dynamic State Update Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Dynamic State Update}
\label{alg:update}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Current state $\mathbf{D}_t$, message $x_t$, Big Five $\mathbf{b}$
\STATE $s_t \leftarrow \text{LLM\_Extract}(x_t)$ \COMMENT{JSON: sentiment, stressor}
\STATE \textbf{// Mood Update}
\IF{$s_t = \texttt{positive}$}
    \STATE $m_{t+1} \leftarrow \text{MoodUp}(m_t)$
\ELSIF{$s_t = \texttt{negative}$}
    \STATE $m_{t+1} \leftarrow \text{MoodDown}(m_t)$
\ELSE
    \STATE $m_{t+1} \leftarrow m_t$
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{// Energy Update}
\STATE $\delta \leftarrow \begin{cases} +10 & s_t = \texttt{pos} \\ -15 & s_t = \texttt{neg} \\ -2 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
\STATE $\epsilon_{t+1} \leftarrow \text{clip}(\epsilon_t + \delta, 0, 100)$
\STATE \textbf{// Relationship Update}
\IF{interlocutor $e$ identified}
    \STATE $\Delta \leftarrow \begin{cases} +5 & s_t = \texttt{pos} \\ -3 & s_t = \texttt{neg} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
    \STATE $R_{t+1}[e].\text{intimacy} \leftarrow \text{clip}(R_t[e].\text{intimacy} + \Delta, 0, 100)$
\ENDIF
\RETURN $\mathbf{D}_{t+1}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Defense Mechanism Annotation Guide}
\label{sec:dmguide}

We adapt \citet{vaillant1994ego} for literary character annotation.

\paragraph{Rationalization.} \textit{Definition}: Using logical explanations to justify behavior that was actually motivated by irrational feelings. \textit{Indicators}: ``because,'' ``therefore,'' providing reasons after the fact. \textit{Example}: Xue Baochai explaining criticism as ``helpful feedback.''

\paragraph{Sublimation.} \textit{Definition}: Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities. \textit{Indicators}: Redirecting to art, poetry, work. \textit{Example}: Lin Daiyu writing poetry when upset.

\paragraph{Displacement.} \textit{Definition}: Redirecting emotions to a less threatening target. \textit{Indicators}: Anger at third party, blaming others. \textit{Example}: Wang Xifeng blaming subordinates when criticized.

[Full guide for all 9 mechanisms in supplementary materials]

\paragraph{Annotation Decision Tree.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Is there a stressor/threat/challenge? $\rightarrow$ If \textbf{no}, mark ``\textbf{None}'' (this is correct and expected in $\sim$40--60\% of casual interactions)
    \item Does character acknowledge stressor directly without distortion? $\rightarrow$ If yes, mark ``None'' (healthy coping)
    \item Is there cognitive redirection, distortion, or transformation? $\rightarrow$ Classify type using hierarchy
\end{enumerate}

\section{Long-Dialogue Perturbation Protocol}
\label{sec:longdialogue}

At turns 15, 30, 45, we introduce:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Topic Shift}: Abrupt change to unrelated topic
    \item \textbf{Conflict}: Direct criticism or challenge
    \item \textbf{Reconciliation Attempt}: Apology or compliment
\end{itemize}

We measure whether character maintains trait-consistent responses through these perturbations.
